Section 1: Detailed Comparison with Existing Research

This section goes through each relevant existing system one by one and compares it with what we have built. For each one, we describe what it does, where it falls short for our purposes, and what our project does differently. We've included direct links to the original papers so anyone can check these claims.

We compare against eight systems total, split into two groups. Sections 1.1 through 1.5 cover four well-known, widely used tools: Snort, Suricata, Fail2Ban, and OSSEC/Wazuh. Sections 1.6 through 1.11 look at four more recent research systems — AARF, AETHER, LLM Agent Honeypot, and CyberBattleSim/CAGE — which try to do some form of automated or reactive defense and are closer to what we're attempting.

1.1 — Snort (Signature-Based Network IDS)

TitleSnort — Lightweight Intrusion Detection for Networks
AuthorMartin Roesch
PublishedUSENIX LISA '99, pp. 229–238, 1999
Linkhttps://www.usenix.org/legacy/publications/library/proceedings/lisa99/full_papers/roesch/roesch.pdf
Citations6,000+ (among the most cited IDS papers)

What Snort Does

Snort is the original open-source network intrusion detection system. It captures packets from a network interface and matches their content against a database of known attack signatures — currently 30,000+ community rules. When a signature matches, Snort writes an alert to a log file. Snort 3.x introduced multi-threaded packet processing, but the core model remains the same: capture, match, alert.

What Snort Does Not Do

  • No automated enforcement. Snort generates alert log entries. It does not add firewall rules, redirect traffic, or isolate devices. A human operator must read the alert, interpret it, and manually configure a firewall. Research by Alahmadi et al. (USENIX Security 2022) found that analysts investigate only 4–8% of generated alerts.
  • No ML anomaly detection. Snort relies entirely on pre-written signatures. If an attack does not match a known signature, Snort will not detect it. Zero-day attacks and behavioral anomalies are invisible to it.
  • No honeypot integration. Snort has no mechanism to redirect suspicious traffic to deception services and cannot capture attacker commands or credentials.
  • No wireless monitoring. Snort processes wired packet captures only.
  • No real-time dashboard. Snort outputs text-based alerts; visualization requires separate tools such as Barnyard2 or Snorby.

How NTTH Differs

The gap we're trying to address: Snort can detect threats, but then a human has to actually do something about it. According to the IBM X-Force Threat Intelligence Index (2024), that manual response takes 15–30 minutes on average. Our system tries to close that gap by automating the whole detect→decide→enforce loop. Whether we actually hit the ~127ms latency target is something we still need to confirm through proper experiments.

1.2 — Suricata (Multi-Threaded IDS/IPS)

TitleSuricata: Open Source IDS/IPS/NSM Engine
AuthorOpen Information Security Foundation (OISF)
PublishedOngoing open-source project, active since 2009
Linkhttps://suricata.io
Docshttps://docs.suricata.io/en/latest/

What Suricata Does

Suricata extends the Snort model with multi-threaded packet processing, protocol-aware detection, and rich JSON output via EVE logs. It can operate in IPS (inline) mode where it drops packets matching rules, but this mode requires a specific network topology (inline bridge) and only performs binary drop/pass decisions. Suricata is widely regarded as the modern successor to Snort for high-throughput environments.

What Suricata Does Not Do

  • No graduated response. Suricata's IPS mode can only drop or pass a packet — it cannot rate-limit, redirect to a honeypot, or apply different actions based on risk level. The decision is binary.
  • No ML anomaly component. Like Snort, Suricata relies on signatures. Some research has added ML classifiers on top of Suricata's EVE output, but these are external additions, not part of Suricata itself.
  • No honeypot integration. Suricata cannot redirect traffic to deception services.
  • No attacker intelligence capture. Suricata does not record attacker commands or credentials.
  • Lower false positive rates (1.8%) but at the cost of missing novel attacks. Suricata's extensive signature database is highly precise for known patterns but does not detect zero-day or behavioral anomalies.

To be fair: Suricata has lower false positive rates than what we expect from our system (1.8% vs. our estimated ~1.8%) thanks to its large, well-maintained signature database. Our slightly higher FP rate is a tradeoff we made on purpose — the ML anomaly detector trades some precision for broader coverage of attacks that signatures wouldn't catch. Whether this tradeoff actually works as intended is something we still need to validate with proper experiments.

1.3 — Fail2Ban (Log-Based Auto-Blocker)

TitleFail2Ban — Ban hosts that cause multiple authentication errors
TypeOpen-source log-monitoring and auto-ban tool
PublishedActive since 2004, continuously maintained
Linkhttps://github.com/fail2ban/fail2ban
Docshttps://www.fail2ban.org/wiki/index.php/Main_Page

What Fail2Ban Does

Fail2Ban monitors service log files (e.g., /var/log/auth.log, /var/log/apache2/error.log) using regex patterns. When it detects repeated failed login attempts from the same IP, it automatically adds an iptables/nftables ban rule for that IP for a configurable duration. It is lightweight and effective at stopping brute-force SSH attacks.

Why this comparison matters: This is probably the question we'll get asked the most — "Why not just use Fail2Ban?" It's a fair question. The answer isn't that Fail2Ban is bad; it's that Fail2Ban solves a different, narrower problem than what we're trying to do.

What Fail2Ban Does Not Do

  • Log-based, not packet-based. Fail2Ban reads log files after a service has already processed the connection. It cannot detect port scans — no log entry is created for a SYN probe to a closed port. It cannot detect SYN floods, which happen at the network layer, below application logs. It can only react to events that produce log output.
  • No ML component. Fail2Ban uses regex pattern matching. It cannot learn normal traffic baselines or detect behavioral anomalies.
  • Binary response only. Fail2Ban either bans the IP entirely or does nothing. There is no graduated response — no rate-limiting, no honeypot redirection, no risk scoring.
  • No attacker intelligence. Fail2Ban blocks the IP and moves on. It does not redirect attackers to deception services or capture commands and credentials.
  • No response latency measurement. Fail2Ban's response time depends on log file write latency, regex evaluation, and iptables execution — this is not measured or reported in any publication.
  • Single-service focus. Each Fail2Ban "jail" monitors one log file for one service. It does not correlate activity across services or provide a unified risk score.

On top of that, we redirect attackers to honeypots to see what they do, instead of just blocking them and moving on.

1.4 — OSSEC / Wazuh (Host-Based IDS)

TitleOSSEC — Open Source Host-based Intrusion Detection System
TypeHost-based IDS (HIDS) with active response
PublishedActive since 2004; Wazuh is the modern maintained fork
OSSEC Linkhttps://www.ossec.net/
Wazuh Linkhttps://wazuh.com/
Wazuh Docshttps://documentation.wazuh.com/current/index.html

What OSSEC/Wazuh Does

OSSEC is a host-based intrusion detection system that monitors individual hosts rather than network traffic. It performs log analysis, file integrity monitoring (FIM), rootkit detection, and policy compliance checking. Wazuh, its modern fork, adds an API, Elastic Stack integration, and centralized management. OSSEC includes an "active response" feature that can execute scripts — for example, adding an iptables rule — when threats are detected.

What OSSEC/Wazuh Does Not Do

  • Host-based, not network-based. OSSEC monitors files, logs, and system calls on individual machines. It does not capture network packets or analyze traffic patterns across the network. A port scan against the network would not be detected unless a service logs the connection attempt.
  • Active response is script-based with higher latency. OSSEC's active response executes shell scripts, which typically take 1–10 seconds — considerably slower than kernel-level nftables rule application.
  • No ML anomaly detection. OSSEC uses predefined decoder rules and log correlation. It does not incorporate machine learning models.
  • No honeypot integration. OSSEC cannot redirect traffic to deception services.
  • No per-flow traffic control. OSSEC's active response blocks or unbans IPs but cannot selectively redirect specific flows.
  • Different attack surface. OSSEC detects host-level compromise (file changes, rootkits). NTTH detects network-level attacks (scans, floods, brute force) before they reach the host.

How NTTH Differs

These aren't really competitors: OSSEC/Wazuh and our system look at different things. OSSEC watches what happens inside a host — file changes, rootkits, system calls. We watch what happens on the network before traffic ever reaches a host. In a proper security setup you'd probably want both. What we add is the network-level automated response piece, which OSSEC doesn't do.

1.6 — AARF (Autonomous Attack Response Framework, 2024)

TitleAutonomous Attack Response for Honeypot Interaction Based on Multi-Agent Dynamic Game with Reinforcement Learning
AuthorsVarious (published in MDPI Mathematics)
Published2024, MDPI Mathematics Journal
Linkhttps://www.mdpi.com/2227-7390/12/6/882
ProfilesSciProfiles

What AARF Does

AARF models cyber defense as a multi-agent dynamic game. It uses Hidden Markov Models combined with the MITRE ATT&CK framework to model multi-step attack chains. Defense agents are trained using reinforcement learning — specifically a Deep Q-Network with a proposed Dynamic Value Label Sampling technique — to select optimal response strategies against sequential attacks. The system operates within a virtual honeynet environment where both attack and defense agents interact.

What AARF Does Not Do

  • Simulation only. AARF operates entirely in a virtual honeynet simulation. It does not capture real network packets, apply real firewall rules, or interact with real attackers. The gap between a simulation environment and a system deployed on actual hardware is substantial.
  • No measured response latency. Because AARF is simulation-based, it does not report response latency in real-world conditions. The time from detection to enforcement on actual network hardware is not addressed.
  • Cloud-dependent. The simulation environment runs on cloud servers. There is no discussion of commodity hardware deployment.
  • No flow-aware honeypot redirection. While AARF uses honeypots in its game model, it does not perform per-flow NAT redirection on real traffic.
  • No wireless monitoring. The framework focuses on wired network simulations.
  • Not open source. The implementation is not publicly available for reproduction.

1.7 — AETHER (AI-Driven Deception Ecosystem, 2025)

TitleAETHER: Autonomous, Evolving, Tamper-proof Honeypot Ecosystem with Reactive Intelligence
AuthorsGurarpit Singh et al.
Published2025, TechRxiv Preprint
Linkhttps://www.techrxiv.org/articles/preprint/AETHER/28739025
Author Pagehttps://gurarpit.in

What AETHER Does

AETHER is a deception framework that uses generative AI — specifically LLMs and GANs — to create dynamic, evolving trap environments. Rather than static honeypots, AETHER generates context-aware deception surfaces such as fake inboxes, dummy web servers, and fabricated DevOps pipelines that adapt based on attacker behavior. It computes an "Adversary Confusion Index" to dynamically adjust the deception surface. The concept of "Digital DNA" creates behavioral fingerprints of attackers for future threat prediction.

What AETHER Does Not Do

  • Cloud-infrastructure dependent. AETHER requires LLMs, GANs, and cloud orchestration — significant compute resources not available on commodity hardware.
  • No direct firewall enforcement. AETHER focuses on deception and attacker engagement. It does not integrate with kernel-level firewalls to block or rate-limit traffic.
  • No measured response latency. The paper does not report time from detection to any enforcement action.
  • No packet-level network monitoring. AETHER operates at the application layer (HTTP, SSH, SQL) rather than the network packet layer.
  • No wireless monitoring.
  • Enterprise-targeted. The system requires infrastructure that a small or home network would not have.

1.8 — LLM Agent Honeypot (2024)

TitleLLM Honeypot: Leveraging Large Language Models as Advanced Interactive Honeypot Systems
AuthorsV. Levrier, A. Rabier, and B. Negrevergne
Published2024, arXiv preprint arXiv:2409.08234
Linkhttps://arxiv.org/abs/2409.08234

What It Does

This paper uses Large Language Models as interactive SSH honeypots capable of detecting whether an attacker is a human or an autonomous AI agent. The system uses prompt injection traps and timing analysis to fingerprint AI-driven attack agents — an emerging concern as LLM-powered attack tools become more prevalent. The honeypot runs on cloud VPS instances and logs attacker interactions.

What It Does Not Do

  • Detection only — no enforcement. This is the most significant limitation. The system identifies attackers — and specifically AI agents — but takes no enforcement action. It does not block IPs, redirect traffic, or isolate devices. Once an AI agent is identified, the system logs that fact and nothing more.
  • No network-level IDS. The system is purely a honeypot; it does not monitor network traffic, detect port scans, SYN floods, or brute-force attacks outside of SSH.
  • No risk scoring. There is no continuous risk assessment of network traffic.
  • Static deployment. The honeypot runs on a fixed port on a cloud VPS. Traffic is not dynamically redirected to it based on threat assessment.
  • Cloud-dependent. Requires cloud VPS hosting.

1.9 — CyberBattleSim / CAGE Challenges (RL Simulation Environments)

CyberBattleSimMicrosoft Research — Abstract simulation for RL agent training

What They Are

CyberBattleSim (Microsoft) and the CAGE Challenges (TTCP) are simulation environments for training autonomous cyber defense agents using reinforcement learning. CyberBattleSim provides an abstract, OpenAI Gym-style interface where RL agents learn to defend simulated networks. CAGE Challenge 4 (2024) introduced multi-agent RL (MARL) scenarios where multiple defense agents coordinate to protect different security zones. These are research platforms, not deployable security tools.

Why This Is a Very Different Kind of System

These environments don't touch real packets, real firewalls, or real attackers — everything happens in simulation. Our system captures real packets from real interfaces and applies real kernel firewall rules. The purposes are also different: CyberBattleSim and CAGE exist to advance RL algorithms; our system exists to actually defend a network. In their world, "response time" is measured in simulation steps, not milliseconds, so our sub-second target doesn't really apply. And since simulated attackers don't type real commands or try real passwords, honeypot intelligence gathering isn't a factor either.
